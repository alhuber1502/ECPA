translation of a latin poem

by the reverend Newton ogle, Dean of Manchester.

oh thou, that prattle on thy pebble way
through my paternal vale do stray,
work thy shallow passage to the sea!
oh, stream, thou speed on
the same as many season go;
but not, alas, to i
remain the feeling that beguile
my early road, when, careless and content,
(lose the hour in pastime innocent)
upon thy bank i stray a playful child;
whether the pebble that thy margin strew,
collect, heedless i throw;
or love in thy translucent wave
my tender shrink foot to lave;
or else ensnare your little fry,
and think how wondrous skill be i!
so pass my boyish day, unknown to pain,
day that will never return again.
it seem but yesterday
i be a child, tomorrow to be grey!
so year succeed year steal silent away.
not fleet thy own current, hurry thou,
roll down to the great sea.
thither oh carry these sad thought; the deep
bury they! — thou, meantime, thy tenor keep,
and wind through the green-wood, cheer,
as erst, my native, peaceful pasture here.
