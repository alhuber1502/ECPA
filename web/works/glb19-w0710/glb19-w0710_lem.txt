on a unfortunate and beautiful woman.

write December 1783. 

oh, mary, when distress and anguish come,
and slow disease prey on thy waste frame;
when every friend, even like thy bloom, be flee,
and want bow low thy unsupported head;
sure sad humanity a tear may give,
and virtue say, live, beauteous sufferer, live!

but shall there one be find, (amid the few
who with compassion thy last pang may view),
one who behold thy error with a tear,
to who the ruin of thy heart be dear,
who fond hope, the ruthful season past,
thy fade virtue may revive at last;
shall such be find — oh! when he see thou lie,
close on every earthly hope thy eye;
when he behold despair, with rueful trace,
mark the strange feature of thy alter face;
when he behold, as painful death draw nigh,
thy pale, pale cheek, thy feeble lift eye,
thy chill, shrink hand, hang down as in despair,
or slow raise, with many a mutter prayer; —
when thus, in early youth, he see thou bend
poor to the grave, and die without a friend;
some sad feeling may unbidden start,
and more than common pity touch his heart!

the eventful scene be close; with pause dread
and sorrow i draw nigh the silent bed;
thy look be calm — thy heart be cold and still,
as if the world have never use it ill;
methinks the last faint smile, with trace weak,
still seem to linger on thy fade cheek.
poor mary! though most beauteous in thy face,
ere sorrow touch it, beam each lovely grace;
yet, oh! thy live feature never wear
a look so sweet, so eloquent before,
as this, which bid all human passion cease,
and tell my pity heart you die in peace!
