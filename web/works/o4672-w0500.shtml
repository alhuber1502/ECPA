TO Mr. William Blackbourn.

Life flies too fast to be Wasted.

1703.
Quae tegit canas modo Bruma valles
Sole vicinos jaculante montes
Deteget rursum —Casimir. Lib. 2. Od. 2.
I.
1 MARK, how it Snows! how fast the Vally fills?
2 And the sweet Groves the hoary Garment wear;
3 Yet the Warm Sun-Beams bounding from the Hills
4 Shall melt the Vail away, and the young Green appear.
[Page 145]
II.
5 But when Old Age has drop't upon your Head
6 Her Silver Frost, there's no returning Sun;
7 Swift rolls our Autumn, swift our Summer's fled,
8 When Youth, and Love, and Spring, and Golden Joys are gone.
III.
9 Then Cold, and Winter, and your Aged Snow
10 Stick fast upon you; not the rich Array,
11 Nor the Green Garland, nor the Rosy Bough
12 Shall cancel or conceal the Melancholy Gray.
IV.
13 The Chase of Pleasure is not worth the Pains,
14 While the Bright Sands of Health run wasting down,
15 And Honour calls you from the softer Scenes
16 To sell the gaudy Hour for Ages of Renown.
V.
17 'Tis but one Youth and short that we can have,
18 And one Old Age dissolves our feeble Frame;
19 But there's a Heavenly Art t' elude the Grave,
20 And with the Heroe-Race Immortal Kindred claim.
[Page 146]
VI.
21 The Man that has his Countries Sacred Tears
22 To drop upon his Herse, has liv'd his Day:
23 Thus, BLACKBOURN, we should leave our Names our Heirs;
24 Old Time and waning Moons sweep all the rest away.
