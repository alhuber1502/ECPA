[Nereides:] Eclogue V.

Mergus. Lycon.
Mer.
1 Lycon begin — begin the mournful Tale;
2 You know what 'tis to love and not prevail:
3 Describe Pasinthas in his daily Moan,
4 How much he lov'd, and how he was undone.
Lyc.
5 Ungrateful Ioessa, vainly coy,
6 And proud of youthful Charms despis'd the Boy;
[Page 23]
7 Has left the calmer Sea's pacifick Arms,
8 Where constant Heat the smiling Ocean warms.
9 To shun the Youth: (such is the Pow'r of Hate)
10 Some windy Bay is now her lone Retreat.
11 In vain Pasinthas sought in ev'ry Cave,
12 In ev'ry Creek, and mark'd each rising Wave;
13 To ev'ry Isle he rov'd with wild Despair,
14 And ask'd, if Ioessa had been there.
15 In vain he has the fruitless Search pursu'd,
16 For she is gone, and will no more be woo'd.
17 Pierc'd with the killing Thought the Lover sighs,
18 And stills the rising Storms with louder Cries:
19 While thus he sadly plains; in mournful Rounds,
20 The Air thro' hollow Rocks repeats the distant Sounds,
21 Each winding Cavern tells the fruitless Care,
22 And ev'ry Rock upbraids the absent Fair;
23 By the sad Echo's which it still returns,
24 It seems to pity, when the Triton mourns:
[Page 24]
25 But the coy Nymph, deaf to the Mer-man's Cry,
26 Is still unmov'd, and makes no kind Reply.
27 While thus Pasinthas plain'd, the Dolphins came,
28 And wept to hear his Moan; the Nereids swam
29 In beauteous Crowds around, and thus they said;
30 "Weep not, fond Triton, for a peevish Maid,
31 " Tho' she is gone let not the Youth despair,
32 "For there are kinder Nymphs, and Nymphs as fair.
33 But, Mergus, Love is deaf as well as blind.
34 The best Advice is thought the most unkind.
35 Restless he goes from the fair pitying Throng
36 To a dark Cave, where Sea-cows lay their young.
37 A silent Grot sad as his Thoughts he found,
38 Where frightful Gloom, and Horrors sate around.
39 There on its slimy Bottom careless laid,
40 He sigh'd and wept; he sigh'd, and then he said:
41 Have I then lov'd to be repaid with Scorn;
42 Ye Gods! 'tis hard, too cruel to be born.
[Page 25]
43 What? — Have I poyson'd too the hated Sea,
44 That Ioessa leaves her home for me?
45 Had you but told; had you your Hatred shown,
46 I would have lov'd unpity'd, and unknown;
47 By my own Flight I had prevented yours,
48 And, banish'd hence, retir'd to distant Shores,
49 Where rigid lasting Cold, and Northern Blasts
50 O'er whiten'd Lands a pearly Shining cast;
51 Where Icy Flakes like floating Isles appear,
52 And fiercely meet; the Noise you'll dread to hear,
53 Nor can your tender Limbs the piercing Climate bear.
54 Muscles in Shoals on mighty Whales attend,
55 Who feed the worthless Fish, and court the puny Friend:
56 Fierce Sharks by gentle Usage are reclaim'd,
57 But Female Pride is savage, and untam'd.
58 Go then, Ingrate, whom Love could never please,
59 To boist'rous Channels, and to foreign Seas,
[Page 26]
60 Where Rocks like you unmov'd with careless Pride
61 Repulse the Waves, and check the rising Tide.
62 Thus the unhappy Youth was heard to moan;
63 The Winds to sigh, the Hollow seem'd to groan,
64 And dropping Tears fell from the weeping Stone.
Merg.
65 Thy Song's more grateful than a Summer's Breeze,
66 Whose cooling Breath, and gentle Fannings please,
67 And move in wanton Rings the listning Seas.
68 Not half so sweet, when first the Morning dawns,
69 Are juicy Oysters, or the luscious Prawns.
70 But now the Sun is dipt in cooling Streams;
71 The twilight is no more; no doubtful Gleams
72 Of weaker Light the flitting Shades divide,
73 But they unmixt prevail, and every Object hide.
74 The Sea is heard with deeper Sound to roar,
75 And slumbring Waters may be said to snore.
76 Each Nymph is stretching on her oozy Bed,
77 And scarce a Fish pops up his sleepy Head;
[Page 27]
78 Those who were clung to Rocks, the shelly Heap
79 Drop from their Hold, and fall into the Deep.
80 Nature her self is still, her Labours cease,
81 And all lies wrapt in Silence, and unactive Ease.
