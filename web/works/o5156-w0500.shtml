VERSES spoken at WESTMINSTER School.

J. F.
1 HOW like you, Sir, the splendor of the day?
2 What! has your lordship not a word to say?
3 Can neither verse, nor prose your praises move?
4 He sure dislikes who cares not to approve.
5 You view with scorn our antiquated ways,
6 Queen Bess's golden rules and golden days.
7 No powder'd liveries attend us here,
8 Hunger's our sauce, and mutton is our cheer.
9 Our worn-out customs may provoke your sport,
10 How long the graces, and the meals how short!
11 Nor can our mouldy college-life afford
12 A bed more fashionable than its board.
13 No state-alcove, no wainscot can you see
14 Of cedar old, or new mahogany:
15 To us, poetic furniture is given,
16 Curtains of night and canopy of heaven:
17 Our youths, whom well-bred gentlemen despise,
18 Sleep with the lamb, as with the lark they rise.
19 Nay, prayers each day (strange things to modern beaux)
20 Open our morning, and our evening close:
21 Nor yet content with what at home we do,
22 Our laws present us to the publick view;
[Page 112]
23 We to the Abbey march in white array
24 Thrice every week, beside each holy day.
25 What boys of rank cou'd brook such hard commands?
26 Like meanest choristers to take their stands,
27 Or penitents, with tapers in their hands?
28 But these objections nobles may disown,
29 Who seldom stoop to wear the daggled gown:
30 The school itself unmannerly they call,
31 Like death a general leveller of all;
32 Which ne'er regards the priv'lege of a peer,
33 What race you spring from, or what arms you bear.
34 Boys on themselves, not ancestors, rely,
35 Distinguish'd by intrinsic quality:
36 A saucy commoner may take his place,
37 Who is a lord, and is to be his grace.
38 Not so at home — there due distinction's made,
39 And full obeisance to degree is paid:
40 Far milder treatment does his honour meet,
41 From handmaid gentle, and from sister sweet:
42 With footmen romps (which finely must improve him)
43 And kiss his cousins that his aunts may love him.
44 There the whole kindred join to form an heir,
45 And uncles, grandsires, grandmothers are there:
46 But oh! th' enchanting blessings who can shew,
47 Which from the kennel, and the stable flow!
48 When honour quits the closet for the fields,
49 And all the student to the sportsman yields.
[Page 113]
50 Perhaps some glorious hunting-match design'd,
51 E'en now, tho' absent, rises to your mind;
52 If not prevented by this luckless day,
53 How had you scower'd o'er hills and dales away,
54 By foxes murder'd glory to obtain,
55 And boast three vixens in a fortnight slain!
56 Or had the generous stag with winged speed
57 Across whole countries urg'd the straining steed,
58 Each Yorkshire Riding might have view'd the race;
59 Your horn perhaps had rung thro' Chevy-Chace.
60 More cou'd I say —
LORD C.
61 — But hold, 'tis time you end,
62 Who for a renegade mistake a friend.
63 And cou'd you think one son so void of grace,
64 T' abjure his Alma Mater to her face?
65 How shou'd not she with irony dispense,
66 Who lends us figures to adorn our sense?
67 Why, 'tis to gain her smiles our parts we prove,
68 To shew our genius is to shew our love:
69 And you the judges, since yourselves inspire,
70 Or our pacific or prolific fire,
71 Be candid, and absolve the general aim,
72 We argue different, but we think the same.
73 Parents, when fondess, or the fashion sway,
74 Will breed their child themselves, the modern way:
75 No pedant schemes, that abject minds controul,
76 Shou'd thwart the native freedom of his soul:
[Page 114]
77 Him their own eye o'erlooks, own modes refine,
78 And master's powder'd ev'ry day to dine.
79 As for his pretty head, mamma takes care
80 The comb's well fix'd, and nicely curl'd the hair,
81 And not one thing, I'll warrant you, breeds there.
82 E'en let the dirty boys, so doom'd, be fools,
83 And walk thro' thick and thin to crowded schools,
84 Lest such rude noise shou'd hurt his tender brain,
85 In his own hall Sir Timothy they train.
86 Moll tells him stories while she sweeps the room,
87 And he imbibes his morals from the groom.
88 At twelve years old the sprightly youth is able
89 To turn a pancake, or dry-rub a table.
90 Soon as the clerk has taught him all he can,
91 They send to London for some abler man.
92 Down comes a Frenchman: Sire, me swear and vow,
93 Me be surpriz'd you make no better bow:
94 But will make you un brave scholar, no fear,
95 Better den my own self, in two, tree year.
96 The knight begins, and in a literal sense,
97 Turns French to English, and makes Latin French.
98 Three years my lady mother has the joy
99 To hear the Frenchman and to see the boy;
100 To her it is a comfort (above all)
101 That Tim should learn so fast, and grow so tall.
102 Kitty, my lady's waiting maid, was sister
103 To Tom the groom, who knew the knight had kiss'd her;
[Page 115]
104 Tom manages his knight at such a rate,
105 He beats the Frenchman, and he marries Kate.
106 So fondly the wise mother lov'd the child,
107 She quite undid him, lest he shou'd be spoil'd.
108 This news the widow of the neighb'ring grange
109 Heard with surprize — But I, said she, will change
110 This unsuccessful method, and my Jerry,
111 I'll answer for't, shall never thus miscarry.
112 Prate with the maid! No — him I'll breed up shyly,
113 And every servant shall respect him highly.
114 No trifling monsieur here shall give advice;
115 I'll have some senior-fellow, grave and wise,
116 From either of our universities.
117 She said — 'Tis done — The honest man with pains
118 Gender and number, mood and tense explains;
119 Jerry goes thro' his daily task and thrives,
120 From in speech be to th' apple-tree arrives.
121 Then studious reads what Belgian authors writ,
122 And drains whole nomenclators for their wit:
123 From thence apace he grows accomplish'd fully,
124 Has read Corderius, and has heard of Tully.
125 Shou'd Oxford next, or Paris be his chance?
126 The last prevails, and he's equip'd for France.
127 He goes — sees every thing that rare and new is,
128 And hunts like any alderman, with Lewis;
129 Till some great fortune, or mamma's command,
130 Again restores him to the British strand,
131 Then, welcome Sir, to bless your native land.
[Page 116]
132 But see the proper vacancy present,
133 And up he comes full fraught for parliament.
134 Then first his noble heart begins to sink,
135 Fain would he speak, but knows not how to think:
136 Howe'er he'll needs launch out beyond his reach,
137 Fer who ne'er made a theme, makes no good speech.
138 Hence the loud laugh, and scornful sneer arise,
139 Hence round and round the piquant raill'ry flies,
140 And thus (sad shame) tho' now he's twenty-four,
141 He's finely lash'd that ne'er was lash'd before.
142 While each mean time, or commoner or peer,
143 Who pass'd the discipline in practice here,
144 Convinc'd applauds the doctor's wholsome plan,
145 Who made the youngster smart to save the man.
146 For what tho' some the good old man desert,
147 Grow learn'd with ease, and grasp the shade of art,
148 For us, we foster here no vain pretence,
149 Nor fill with empty pride the void of sense;
150 We rise with pains, nor think the labour light
151 To speak like Romans, and like Romans write.
152 'Tis ours to court with care the learned throng,
153 To catch their spirit as we gain their tongue;
154 To enjoy the charms in Caesar's works that shine,
155 And learn to glow at Virgil's lofty line.
156 'Twas thus you mov'd, and thus in riper years,
157 With such superior lustre fill your spheres;
158 'Twas thus you learn'd to rise, nor can you blame
159 If as we tread your steps we hope your fame.
[Page 117]
160 And oh! may Westminster for ever view
161 Sons after sons succeed, and all like you;
162 May every doubt your great examples clear,
163 And Education fix her empire here.
