curious story, for the Pennsylvania magazine

three justice (so sais my tale)
once meet upon the public weal.
for learning, law, and part profound,
their fame be spread the county round;
each by his wondrous art can tell,
of thing as strange, as Sydrophel;
or by the help of sturdy ale,
so clever can tell a tale,
that half the gape stander by,
will laugh aloud. the rest will cry.
or by the help of noble wine,
will knotty point so nice define,
that in a instant right be wrong,
yet do not hold that station long,
for while they talk of wrong and right
you’d see the question out of sight.
each know by practice where to turn
to ev’ry powerful page in burn,
and can help of note and book
talk law like Littleton and coke.
each know by instinct when and where,
a farmer catch, or kill’d a hare.
can tell if any man have get
one hundred pound, per ann, or not.
or what be great, can divine,
if it be only ninety nine,
for when the hundred want one
they take away the owner gun.
know by the leer of a eye
if girl have lose their chastity,
and if they have not — will divine
some way to make their virtue shine.
these learned brother be assemble,
(at which the country fear’d and tremble)
a warrant send to bringbefore ’em,
one farmer short who dwell at Shoreham,
upon a great and heavy charge,
which we’ve recite here at large,
that those who be not there may read,
in after day the mighty deed.

Viz.
“that he” the foresaid “farmer short
“be by the devil move, have not,
“one hundred pound per annum get.
“that have not (in form likewise)
“the fear of god before his eye,
“by force and arm do keep and cherish,
“within the fore say town and parish,
“against the statute so provide.
“a dog. and there the dog abide.
“that he, this dog, do then, and there,
“pursue and take and kill a hare.
“which treason be, or some such thing,
“against our sovereign lord the king.”
the constable be bid to jog
and bring the farmer — not the dog.
but fortune who perpetual wheel,
grind disappointment sharp as steel,
on purpose to attack the pride,
of those who over other ride,
so nice bring the matter round,
that farmer short can not be find,
which plung’d the bench in so much doubt
they know not what to go about.
but after pond’ring pro, and con,
and mighty reas’nings thereupon,
they find on open of the law,
that he, the dog, aforesaid be,
by be privy to the fact,
within the meaning of the act,
and since the master have withdraw,
and be the lord know whither go,
they judg’d it right, and good in law,
that he, the dog, shall answer for
such crime, as they by proof can show,
be act by himself and co.  
the constable again be send,
to bring the dog; or dread th’event.
poor porter right before the door,
be guard of his master’s store;
and as the constable approach’d he,
he catch he by the leg and broach’d he;
for porter think (if dog can think)
he come to steal his master chink.
the man, by virtue of his staff,
bid people help; not stand and laugh.
on which a mighty route begin,
some blam’d the dog; and some the man.
some say he have no business there,
some say, he’d business ev’ry where;
at length the constable prevail,
and those who will not help be jail;
and take porter by the collar
command all the guard to follow.
the justice receive the felon,
with great form then i cantell on,
and quit of their wine and punch,
begin upon he. all at once.
at length a curious quibble rise,
how far the law can interpose,
for it be prove and right too,
that he, the dog, do not pursue
the hare, with any ill intent,
but only follow by the scent;
and she, the hare, by run hard,
through’ hedge and ditch without regard,
plung’d in a pond and there be drown’d
and by a neighb’ring justice find;
wherefore, though’ he, the hare annoy,
it can’t be say that he destroy’d;
it even can’t be prov’d hebeat she,
and ‘to destroy’ must mean to ‘eat she.’
do you ne’er see a gamester strike,
with all the symptom of ill luck,
or mark the visage which appear,
when even hope herself despair;
so look’d the bench, and every brother,
sad picture draw of one another;
till one more learn than the rest,
rise up, and thus the court address’d.
“why gentleman, i’ll tell you how,
“you may clear up this matter now,
“for i be of opinion strong
“the dog deserve, and shall be hang.
“i’ll prove it by as plain a case,
“as be the nose upon your face.”
“now if, suppose, a man, or so,
“shall be oblig’d, or not, to go,
“about, or not about a case,
“to this, or that, or t’other place;
“and if another man for fun
“shall fire a pistol, (viz.) a gun
“and he, the first, by know not,
“that he, the second man, have shoot,
“shall undesign’dly meet the bullet,
“against his throat (in greek) the gullet,
“and get such mischief by the hit,
“as shall unsense he of his wit,
“and if that, after that, he die,
“D’ye think the other mayn’t be try:
“most sure he must, and hang’d, because
“he fire his gun against the law;
“for ’tis a case most clear and plain,
“have a. not shoot B. have not be slay.
“so have the dog not chas’d the hare,
“she never have be drown — that’s clear.”
this logic, rhetoric, and wit,
so nice do the matter hit,
that porter — though’ unheard be cast,
and in a halter breath’d his last.
the justice, adjourn’d to dine
and whet their logic up with wine.
