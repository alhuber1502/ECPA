the farmer winter evening, a poem.

to the nymph i never see.

far be the pleasure of the day,
and mirth and festive joy from i,
when cold December nip the plain,
or freeze January reign.
far be the huntsman noisy horn,
and courser fleet through thicket bear,
swift as the wind, and far the sight,
of snowy mountain, sad white;
but thou, o night, with sober charm,
shall clasp i in thy sable arm.
for thou i love the winter eve,
the noisy day for thou i leave.
beneath some mountain tower height,
in cottage low i hail the night,
where jovial swain, with heart sincere,
and timely mirth dishearten care:
each tell his tale, or chant a song
of she for who he sigh so long;
of Clara fair, or Flora coy,
disdain still her shepherd boy,
while near the hoary head sage,
recall the day of youthful age,
describe his course of manly year,
his journey through this vale of tear;
how champion he with champion meet,
and fierce they do combat it,
until envious night in ebon chair,
urge fast on her chariotteer,
and rob he, o for shame, of glory
and feat fit for renown in story. —
thus spend in tale the evening hour,
and quaff juice of sober power,
which handsome Kate with malt do steep,
to lead on balmy visaged sleep,
while her neat hand the milk pail strain,
a savoury supper for the swain.
and now the moon exalt high,
give lustre to the earth and sky,
and from the mighty ocean glass,
reflect the beauty of her face:
about her orb you may behold,
a thousand star of burnish gold,
which slow to the west retire,
and lose a while their glitter fire.

o can i here find my abode,
and live within this fancy wood,
with thou the week and year to pass,
my pretty rural shepherdess;
with thou the cool spring to sip,
or live upon thy damask lip:
then sacred grove, and shade divine,
and all Arcadia shall be i.
steep i, steep i some poppy deep
in beechen bowl, to bring on sleep;
love have my mind in shackle keep,
thrice the cock crew, nor once i sleep.
o gentle sleep, wrap i in dream,
of field and wood, and run stream;
of river wide, and castle rare,
and be my lovely Flora there:
a large draught, a large bowl
to gratify my drowsy soul;
"a large draught be yet in store,
perhaps with this you wake no more."
then i my lovely maid shall see thou
drink the deep stream of Lethe,
where now dame Arethusa scatter
her soft stream with Alpheus 'water,
to forget her earthly care,
lose in Lethe, lose in year!
and i too will quaff the water,
lest it shall be say, o daughter
of my giddy, wander brain,
i sigh for one I have never see.
