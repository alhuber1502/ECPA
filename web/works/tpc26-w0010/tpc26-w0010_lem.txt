the injur’d ghost of liberty

to his grace the duke of Grafton

the injure ghost of liberty in 1769 at the bar of the H— of C—

i 

when all be close in dreadful night,
and most thing wish’d repose,
forth from it’s tomb the ghastly spright
of liberty arise.

ii.

her feature all benumb’d with woe,
her skin be pale with care,
her languid limb be parch’d with grief,
that once so pliant be.

iii.

with mournful step to find her spouse,
and seek for freedom there,
she wander’d to that fatal house
from whence her sorrow be.

iv.

she touch’d the bar, and give a groan,
bid sergeant hold her shroud;
she look’d about, and shake her head,
and thus bewail’d aloud:

v. 

mr. speaker, i come here to wish,
with patience you wou’d hear,
’tis plaintiff liberty that speak,
the house will lend a ear.

vi.

hear, hear — then see the breathless form,
which thy misdeed have bring,
hear, hear — behold that empty shade
which thy misjudgement wrought.

vii.

look here, and see the piteous shape
that once be fond to live:
say, monster, why do you destroy
that life ye’re here to give.

viii.

the method take be horrid foul,
through Bute and Grafton’s will,
the prince bid the dame to know,
she wou’d be ruler still.

ix.

let Bute himself be forc’d to see
the deadly waste he’s make
to blast the fruit that’s britain’s growth
in liberty’s fair mead.

x. 

then let he feel with tortur’d heart
the stroke he late give,
for briton will lament the deed,
and rue it to their grave.

xi.

this foreign princess vow’d revenge
on wilkes’s curse plot,
nor wou’d she rest one night or day
till full revenge she get.

xii.

then Grafton come, and promise much
if Grenville will firm stand,
he’d carry through the wrong begin
with high despotic hand.

xiii.

how cou’d you (none but you can do)
cut off my bloom so soon,
and let my last night come on
amid its perfect noon.

xiv.

hear, hear — thus fall that bulwark law
which hold i fast in hand,
and with it dragg’d i to its grave,
and bid i leave the land.

XV. 

here then i stand, and firm ask
what right you have to act
and judge against the well know law
of freedom and of fact.

xvi.

what then be gain’d by Blackstone’s book,
or cull’d from Nugent’s law,
when contradict by themselves,
it matter not a straw.

xvii.

the treasury band lead up the van;
then they be sure to win,
the major part go out with north,
the minor stay within.

xviii.

how can you vow a patriot part,
and yet that vow forsake;
how cou’d you think to win my heart,
yet cause that heart to break.

xix.

why do you tell to all the world
their freedom you’d protect;
yet true prove those gild word
to be of none effect.

xx.

oft have i hear this house declare,
that liberty shall live,
when by your wound word you mean
nought else but to deceive.

xxi.

oft have i on my bend knee
submissive come to know
your will — what not? — to please that will
be forc’d to undergo.

xxii.

where be the few who lov’d i dear?
have they forsake the land?
sure they have not be brib’d by gold,
nor buy by Grafton’s hand.

xxiii.

yes, yes, the tale be sure true,
or else no force cou’d do’t;
for britain’s liberty to fall
a sacrifice to Bute.

xxiv.

where be those man who dar’d destroy
the nation’s right and i;
to Bute — the sacrifice be make,
the shame o grafton’s thy.

xxv.

what more, you fiend, ye’d wish to do?
what treach’rous ill impart?
’Twas you, two hundred twenty-two,
that stabb’d i to the heart.

xxvi.

the tribe of law step forth, and take
those wicked deed in hand,
decree the law of parliament,
be law for all the land.

xxvii.

the freeman’s vote be soon destroy’d,
and with it go his right;
the great number be the less,
the less choose the knight.

xxviii.

thus be i slay, and thus i fall,
o curs’d decree of law,
thus from the kingdom i’m ordain’d
for ever to withdraw.

xxix.

the nation lov’d i to the heart,
and i to it belong’d,
but part thus by shameful act,
the kingdom have be wrong’d.

xxx.

why for such hardship be i nurs’d
in britain’s fair isle
or say perfidious why these limb
be suffer’d thus to spoil.

xxxi.

pause on the wicked deed ye’ve do.  —
dream of the fatal change!
your crime be number’d in the book
which justice shall avenge.

xxxii.

say, where your wicked soul will lodge,
when from your body flee!
think on those pang ye’re soon to feel
in that torment bed!

xxxiii.

may spectre stare you in the face!
may horror guard you round!
may conscience ne’er forsake such fiend,
but all your thought confound!

xxxiv.

let ægypt’s plague invent distress,
and every art to teaze,
till every briton’s wrong redress’d,
ne’er let those torment cease.

XXXV. 

i be a ghost ’tis true, you see,
i come to haunt the house;
your wicked deed have wrought the change,
a lion to a mouse.

xxxvi 

but hark! the midnight bell have toll’d,
to call i to my home;
atone for this your black offence,  —
remember i when go!
