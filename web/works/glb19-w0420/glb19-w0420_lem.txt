Milton.

on the bust of Milton, in youth and age, at stourhead.

in youth.

Milton, our noble poet, in the grace
of youth, in those fair eye and cluster hair,
that brow untouched by one faint line of care,
to mar its openness, we seem to trace
the front of the first lord of human race,
'amid thy own paradise portray so fair,
ere sin or sorrow scathe it: such the air
that character thy youth. shall time efface
these lineament as crowd care assail!
it be the lot of fall humanity.
what boot it! arm in adamantine mail,
the unconquerable mind, and genius high,
right onward hold their way through weal and woe,
or whether life brief lot be high or low!

in age.

and be thou he, now "fall on evil day,"
and change indeed! yet what do this sink cheek,
these thin lock, and that calm forehead speak!
a spirit reckless of man blame or praise, —
a spirit, when thy eye to the noon blaze
their dark orb roll in vain, in suffer meek,
as in the sight of god intent to seek,
'amid solitude or age, or through the way
of hard adversity, the approve look
of its great master; while the conscious pride
of wisdom, patient and content to brook
all ill to that sole master task apply,
shall show before high heaven the unaltered mind,
Milton, though thou be poor, and old, and blind!
