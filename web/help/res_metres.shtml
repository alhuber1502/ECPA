Metres Database

The Eighteenth-Century Poetry Archive Metres Database offers a metre-based access point to the poetry corpus, and is the companion to the ECPA rhyme-index. Where the rhyme-index gives a rhyme-analytical view of the corpus, the Metres Database focusses on metrical form: foot type and foot count, line length, stanza form, rhyme scheme, and poetic form or genre, both across the whole corpus and in the practice of individual poets. It embraces data-visualisation and interactive filtering so the formal structure of eighteenth-century verse can be explored rather than merely looked up. The database has been developed with Google charts and Cytoscape.js.

Interfaces

The Metres Database comprises a corpus overview with a faceted browser, a full-page Network Explorer, poet-based pages, and form / genre profile pages. The Network Explorer is reached from the link at the top right of the page; a poet's page is reached from any results table (by clicking an author name) or from a poet node in a network; a form profile is reached by selecting a single form or genre (for example clicking a form in a chart or a companion form on another profile). The data underlying each panel is downloadable as CSV via the button at the top right of the panel; on the corpus page the download always reflects the current facet selection, i.e. exactly the data behind what is on screen.

Corpus overview and faceted browser

The left-hand facets let you filter the corpus by foot type, foot count, line-length profile, rhyme scheme, stanza form, and poetic form / genre. Facets combine (e.g. iambic × tetrameter × abab), and because several categories are recorded as sets (a ballad-metre poem is both tetrameter and trimeter), a poem is matched if it carries any of the selected values for a facet. Selected facets appear as removable chips, and the address bar updates as you filter, so any view can be bookmarked or cited. The summary tiles and the distribution charts (foot type, foot count, line length, form / genre, stanza, poem length) recompute live for the current selection, as does the matching poems table, which is sortable and carries a filter box that searches across all of its columns at once.

Network Explorer

The Network Explorer is a full-page interactive graph. A dropdown switches between four views. The two corpus views, metre and form / genre and form and form, are drawn by association rather than by raw frequency: because nearly nine in ten poems are iambic, a plain co-occurrence graph would simply restate that everything connects to iambic. Instead, each edge measures how far two things go together relative to chance, so a red link means a pairing far more common than expected (the anapaestic lilt of song and comic verse, the trochaic cast of hymns and odes) and a blue link one far rarer than expected (blank verse shunning short tetrameter lines, song shunning the stately pentameter); the thicker the edge, the stronger the surprise. Pairings that fall close to chance are not drawn at all, so every edge you see is a genuine over, or under, representation rather than an artefact of how common the two things are. Form nodes are tinted by the foot type they lean towards (pale blue for iambic, green for anapaestic, orange for trochaic), so metrical families and the forms that defect from the iambic default stand out at a glance.

The two poet views turn the same lens on authors. Poet and poet (metrical twins) draws a link only between poets who each count the other among their nearest by metrical fingerprint, so the graph resolves into genuine twin pairs and clusters that share a signature rather than a uniform mesh; node size shows how many twins a poet has. Poet and metrical signature is a bipartite map joining each prolific poet to the foot types, foot counts and forms they use distinctively (far more than the corpus average), so shared signatures pull poets together, e.g. the song-writers around song, the couplet-poets around the heroic couplet. Because almost every poem is iambic, the iambic foot is no poet's distinctive trait and never appears here; only the non-default feet, the anapaestic and trochaic, surface as foot-type signatures (dactylic verse, just a handful of poems in the whole corpus, is too rare to register). Sliders control how many nodes are shown and, for the corpus views, the minimum support behind an edge. Hovering highlights a node's neighbourhood, and clicking a node jumps to it: a metre or form node filters the corpus overview, a poet node opens that poet's page. You can drag to pan and scroll to zoom, with a minimap.

A canonical poet may nonetheless sit at the metrical margins: Alexander Pope, for instance, writes so squarely in the period's default measure (the iambic pentameter heroic couplet) that, once ubiquitous traits are down-weighted, no other poet counts him among their nearest, so he has no metrical twin and does not appear in the twin network. For exactly this reason the poet views offer a focus poet search: type any poet to redraw the graph as their metrical ego-network, the chosen poet (in gold) surrounded by the poets nearest to them, with node size showing closeness. It is the quickest way to place a specific poet, whether or not they are metrically central.

Poet pages

A poet page gives a metre-centric view of an individual author's practice. It opens with summary tiles and a poet's metrical fingerprint: the distribution of foot types, foot counts, rhyme-scheme types, stanza forms, line-length profiles, rhyme schemes, poetic forms, and poem lengths. The distinctiveness panel compares the poet's form usage against the corpus average, so what is characteristic of the poet stands out. The metrical neighbours panel lists the poets whose metrical fingerprint is most similar (by cosine similarity, weighted so that ubiquitous traits such as iambic pentameter do not dominate). A sortable, searchable and downloadable table of the poet's poems links back into each poem, and to the poet's rhyme-index profile, so rhyme and metre can be studied together.

Form / genre profiles

A form profile gathers everything the corpus holds about a single poetic form or genre, the heroic couplet, the ode, the ballad, and so on. It opens with the form's typical recipe, expressed as a plain sentence (for example "most often iambic pentameter in couplet form, rhyming aa") and a strictness reading giving the share of poems that follow that recipe. The recipe panels then show how the form distributes across foot type, foot count, line length, stanza and rhyme, alongside a switchable cross-tab that pairs two of those dimensions and colours each cell by association (red for more than chance, blue for less). Further panels list the form's signature poets (its most prolific practitioners, and those for whom it is most distinctive), its companion forms and genres (the tags these poems most often also carry), any variants that depart from the recipe, and a searchable, downloadable table of the poems themselves. A profile is reached by selecting a single form or genre; a link in the poems panel reopens the same selection in the faceted browser, so further facets can be added without losing the combinatorial search.

Metrical categories

The classifications used throughout the database derive from the ECPA encoding taxonomy.1 The principal categories are:

Foot type

The metrical foot, notated with ˘ (metrically non-prominent / unstressed) and ′ (metrically prominent / stressed). Those attested in the corpus are iambic (˘′), trochaic (′˘), anapaestic (˘˘′) and dactylic (′˘˘).

Foot count

The number of feet per line: monometer (1), dimeter (2), trimeter (3), tetrameter (4), pentameter (5), hexameter (6) and heptameter (7). Many poems alternate line lengths (e.g. common / ballad metre), so a poem may carry more than one foot count.

Line-length profile

The syllable count per line. A single number (e.g. 10) marks a uniform line length (here decasyllabic, typical of iambic pentameter); a dotted sequence (e.g. 8.6.8.6) marks alternating line lengths, in this case common metre.

Stanza form

Recorded by number of lines: couplet (2), triplet (3), quatrain (4), quintet (5), sestet (6), septet (7), octet (8) and longer forms, together with named stanzas such as sesta rima and the Pindaric strophe, antistrophe and epode.

Rhyme scheme and rhyme-scheme type

The rhyme scheme facet gives the literal scheme (aa, abab, …). The related rhyme-scheme type (charted on poet pages and on the rhyme-index) records the underlying arrangement: pair (aabb), cross (abab), envelope (abba), tail (aabccb), intermittent (xaxa), continuous (aaa) and cinquain (abaab).

Poetic form / genre

A single flat vocabulary of formal and thematic tags, from stanzaic and metrical forms (heroic couplet, sonnet, blank verse, Spenserian stanza, ballad) through kinds and genres (ode, elegy, satire, epistle, hymn) to modes (translation, imitation, paraphrase). A poem commonly carries several such tags.

Metrical pattern

The abstract metrical template of a line, e.g. ˘′|˘′|˘′|˘′|˘′/ for iambic pentameter, where | marks a foot boundary and / the line end. (Note that this is the nominal template; it does not record realised syllable-by-syllable scansion.)

Notes on the data

All categories are drawn from poem-level metrical metadata. Where a category was not assigned, a poem is shown under (unclassified) for that facet, with transparent counts. Prose pieces, having no metrical line structure, are excluded from the database. ECPA poems are edited as they appear within their source editions and were not assigned systematic composition or publication dates; the database therefore offers no chronological or timeline views.

Future work

One of the most beneficial side effects of exploratory analysis of this kind is that it tends to reveal inconsistencies and errors in the underlying data. The Metres Database is no exception, and we apologise for any errors, which we will correct as we become aware of them. We appreciate reports of any issues, as well as suggestions for additional views or feedback on the usefulness of the interface, to the editor. Thank you!


1 The full encoding taxonomy of formal and metrical categories is maintained in the ECPA encodingDesc (ECEP.met.*), which underlies both the archive's TEI encoding and this database.
